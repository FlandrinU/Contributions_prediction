4.1 Filtering RLS data

-> Faire un schéma des étapes de la méthodes

Filter misidentifications

Remove all observations with misidentifications: “spp.”, “sp.”, “(cf)”, “hybrid”, “Unidentified”, “?”, “!”, “/”, “New”, “green form”

Keep only fish abundance assessement from RLS protocol

select “method 1, block 1 and 2” in RLS metadata (fish assessment in 2 bands of 5mx50m)

filter duplicates

Same information recorded twice, by two different divers (same survey ID, block, species, total, and size) -> keep only one observation for each.

filter lines without fish

remove line with total = 0

filter fishes difficult to spot consistently with UVC

-Remove Elasmobranchs (different behaviours, shy or not, …)

-Remove anguiliforms: 16 families according to WORMS: c( “Anguillidae”, “Chlopsidae”, “Colocongridae”, “Congridae” , “Derichthyidae” , “Heterenchelyidae” , “Moringuidae” , “Muraenesocidae” , “Muraenidae” , “Myrocongridae” , “Nemichthyidae” , “Nettastomatidae” , “Ophichthidae” , “Protanguillidae” , “Serrivomeridae” , “Synaphobranchidae”)

-Remove cryptobenthic families: c(“Aploactinidae”, “Apogonidae”, “Blenniidae”, “Bythitidae”, “Callionymidae”, “Chaenopsidae”, “Creediidae”, “Dactyloscopidae”, “Gobiesocidae”, “Gobiidae”, “Grammatidae”, “Labrisomidae”, “Opistognathidae”, “Plesiopidae”, “Pseudochromidae”, “Syngnathidae”, “Tripterygiidae”)

Checking size

-Filling values in Maximum length data: missing values are completed by applying the mean of the genus or the family (if no species in the same genus)

-Remove juveniles: all fish of 2.5 cm, and fish of 5 cm if the maximum length of the species is > 25 cm

-Remove abnormal size estimation: fish with a max length < 3.75, individuals for which size_class > 2*(Maximum length) or (Maximum length+50) => we removed all the surveys with size_class errors.

Filter outliers surveys

To avoid over-estimating fish abundance due to temporary fish aggregations or large roaming schools, we excluded surveys with a total biomass over 500 kg/500m² or an abundance exceeding 10,000 individuals.

Elasmobranch data

All Elasmobranch occurences are kept for the surveys meeting all the above conditions (Elamsobranch abundance isn’t used)

Merge traits

To avoid taxonomic mismatch, we associated the species name of all databases and RLS data, with the accepted scientific name of rfishbase and its SpecCode (unique ID). All data base have been then merged using this unique ID.

Traits inference

Assess the performance of missforest on prediction: On 100 iterations, we create a trait dataset with 20% of NAs randomly placed. We used MissForest package (ntree = 100, max_iter = 10) to infer the created missing values and evaluated the prediction performance. Categorical variables were evaluate with accuracy (proportion of good prediction) and numerical variables were evaluated with R-squared correlation, over the 100 iterations. The expected proportion of error in our final dataset was calculated as the proportion of missing values multiplied by the potential error of missforest ( Prop_NA * (1-R²) ). We choose to fill gaps on variables with reasonable risk of error . To fill the gaps, we ran 100 missforest models on the dataset and check the consistency of all models. Categorical values were kept if more than 80% of models converged to the same result. The median of the 100 numerical values were kept if the coefficient of variation (sd/mean) < 0.2, i.e if 1-CV > 0.8.

Inference of benthic composition -> to reformulate

Benthic composition is assessed during RLS surveys using photo-quadrats of the substrate. Twenty evenly-spaced photos are recorded along the survey transect and later processed using SQUIDLE+ software (https://squidle.org/). Points are randomly overlaid on photographs and users identify the organisms into broad functional groups (and sometimes to the lowest taxonomic resolution possible) that the points land on. This provides percent cover data for benthic organisms such as corals, macroalgae, sponges, etc. Here, we binned the groups into 9 broader benthic groups that can be consistently applied (globally): coral, algae, coralline algae, seagrass, other sessile invertebrates (e.g., ascidians, sponges), coral rubble, rock, sand, and microalgal mats (e.g., cyanobacteria, encrusting algae films). We first applied an arcsine transformation (inverse Sine of square-root transformed values) to the proportional benthic cover data, which were highly right skewed. This is similar to a log transformation but more appropriate for proportional data, which are bounded between 0 and 1 [REF]. In the tropical zone, we used the available socio-environnmentales covariates to infer the 29% of missing values on benthic composition. We first applied a PCoA on these covariates, except spatial information (latitude, longitude, depth), to reduce 63 variables into 20 composite dimensions. We then used these 20+3 spatial dimensions to infer the arcsine transformed benthic composition. We tested the accuracy of the imputation by cross validation with the observed data. We randomly deleted 30% of true observations, imputed those values, and checked the correlation between the true and the imputed values. Correlations were reasonably good for all categories (R-squares between 0.4 and 0.7), with very good predictions for algae and coral cover (R-squares ~0.7 for each).
