Abstract

To what extent has humanity degraded ecosystems? This is a crucial question we need to ask ourselves if we are to reconcile our relationship with nature and imagine a more desirable future. Although everyone has heard anecdotal accounts of once-abundant species from previous generations, systematic quantification of ecosystem degradation over time is difficult to achieve due to limited historical data. Instead, it is possible to use the most pristine ecosystems known today to estimate the lower bound of our current impact on nature. By measuring 22 potential contributions of fish communities to nature and people in over 2000 tropical reefs, we have explored the impact of anthropogenic activities and conservation measures on fish communities. By explaining these contributions by more than 40 environmental, habitat and human covariates, we conducted counterfactual scenarios on unprotected and protected reefs to measure the effect of anthropogenic activities and conservation measures on contributions to Nature and People. We show that anthropogenic activities have massively reduced the biomass and diversity of fish communities on tropical reefs, reducing the total measured contributions by an average of 10%. Despite a non-negligible impact on biomass and IUCN species richness, conservation efforts to date have only been able to offset the decline by a few percent. Our results show that despite the undoubted benefits of current protected areas, reef protection cannot and will not compensate for all our extraction, pollution and ecosystem destruction. A change in our relationship with nature is therefore urgent and imperative if we are to imagine an enviable future for nature and people.

What is the impact of human activities on ecosystems, (compared with known minimum impact conditions)? Given current human populations, what gains have protected areas brought?

  • “Silent spring” Rachel Carson (1962) -> serious threats to terrestrial ecosystems arising from our extensive farming practices.

  • Aujourd’hui, extinction des oiseaux, insectes, ….

  • “Le monde du silence” Jacques Cousteau (1956): montre la richesse des océans, qui depuis n’a pas cessée d’être exploitée et dégradée.

  • = Man has a profound and pervasive impact on every ecosystem on Earth

Contributions

  • Chute de biomasse générale et dégradation des ecosystèmes -> ils ont aujourd’hui du mal à se maintenir et les contributions à l’homme ne cessent de diminuer.

  • Tellement qu’aujourd’hui les ecosytèmes terrestres sont majoritairement controller en agrosystèmes, optimisés pour la production.

  • Ces contributions de la nature, quelles soient bénéfiques à la nautre, aux sociétés, ou faisant partie de la culture des population locale, représentent le lien que nous entretenons avec la Nature. Le NFF nous questionne sur quelle relation nous voulons.

Identify inherent and environmental effects, and possible action levers

  • pour se projetter, nous devons comprendre quels sont les drivers des contributions.

  • il existe des drivers inhérents (gradient latitudinal, …), des modifications à long terme comme le changement climatique, et des drivers directs et immédiats comme nos activités d’exploitations et pollutions.

  • Certains dangers sont à mitiger sur le long terme comme l’effet du changement climatique, mais on a aussi des leviers que l’on peut changer tout de suite: nos degrés d’exploitation, de pollution, … ie.e. notre relation avec la Nature.

  • Par exemple, les PA strictes semblent être un moyen efficace de préserver une partie des ecosystèmes ou de mitiger l’effet de l’homme, mais nous ne savons pas bien quels effets cela a sur diverses contributions à grande échelle.

  • De plus, malgré les résultats interessant des PA par rapport aux zones non protégées, la plupart des ecosystèmes sont soumis à des pressions humaines, et nous pouvons nous demander à quel point des PA protège les ecosystèmes de la pression humaine?

  • Comprendre l’effet des réserves sur les contributions permettrait de mieux ajuster les plans de management pour favoriser les relation homme-Nature, et permettrait de savoir ce à quoi on peut s’attendre en terme de contribution.

Infer and predict community contributions directly through SDM or jSDM

  • Pour mieux comprendre l’influence anthropique sur les communeauté et leurs contributions on peut essayer de modéliser directement les contributions à partir du contexte socio-environnemental.

  • Species Distribution Model directement appliqués aux métriques des communeautés comme les contributions, comme suggéré par Thuillier 2024 (+ Poggiato 2023)

  • On peut même utiliser des jSDM pour mieux prendre en compte les covariations entre les contributions.

Conterfactual scenarios

  • pour comprendre l’effet des réserves et de l’homme sur les contributions, les données temporelles quantitatives sont généralement manquantes ou difficile à exploiter.

  • Mais en utilisant les jSDM, nous pouvons comparer des sites protégés ou non, pour comprendre l’effet de la protection en tenant compte du contexte socio-environnemental.

  • En utilisant la diversité des contextes d’un ecosystème observé aujourd’hui, nous pouvons nous demander quel serait l’état d’un ecosystème donné, s’il avait suivi une autre trajectoire de pression anthropique, par exemple d’un ecosytème protégé. C’est ce qu’on appel un contrefactuel (Caldwell 2024).

  • de cette manière, nous pouvons regarder quel a été l’effet de la protection sur un ecosystèmes en prenant en compte ses spécificités locales, et regarder le gains en contributions apporté.

  • à l’inverse, nous pouvons observer l’effet de la présence humaine en prenant en compte le contexte envronnemental, pour comprendre quelle a été notre empreinte écologique et quelles contributions en patissent.

  • Aujourd’hui, aucun système n’a été épargner de l’activité humaine. Mais en étudiant les contributions dans les ecosystèmes les moins touchés par l’homme, i.e. “pseudo-pristine”, on peut estimer l’effet qu’a eu a minima l’homme sur les écosystèmes anthropisés.

  • Ainsi on peut se demander: Quels sont les bénéfices des réservess en terme de contributions ? quel serait les niveaux de contribution des communeautés si on était resté au minimum de pression anthropique que l’on connait actuellement ? i.e. quel a été l’impact de l’humain pour l’instant par rapport aux conditions minimum d’impact connues. Est ce que les plans de conservations apportent des bénéfices du même ordre de grandeur que les pertes angendrées par la dégradation anthropique ?

  • Les récifs tropicaux sont de parfaits systèmes biologiques pour ce type d’étude:

    • menacés par le changement climatique, mais aussi par l’exploitationn humaine, pollution, …

    • ce sont des écosystèmes qui soutiennent encore les populations locales, avec 1 milliard de personnes qui en dépendent, pour la pêche, nutriments, mais aussi comme inspiration culturelle…

2 Draft Introduction

In 1962, Rachel Carson denounced in her book Silent Spring the serious threats posed to terrestrial ecosystems by intensive agricultural practices. More than sixty years after her warnings, land-use changes -driven largely by intensive agriculture- have led to the extinction of over 75% of insect biomass (Hallman 2017) and at least a quarter of the abundance of common birds in Europe (Rigal 2023). A few years earlier, Jacques-Yves Cousteau introduced the general public to the richness of marine ecosystems in The Silent World (1956). Since then, these marine environments have faced relentless exploitation that continues to intensify [REF]. These archive documents remind us that within a few decades, nearly all terrestrial and marine ecosystems have undergone dramatic transformations, witnessing the pervasive and massive impact of human activities on the biosphere. Today, human activities have caused a drastic decline in global biomass and a drop in the capacity of ecosystems’ ability to maintain their core functions. The contributions they provide to nature and people keep declining [REF Diaz]. On land, only a small proportion of the human population depends on wild ecosystems, as most have been transformed into agrosystems optimised for production. Conversely, marine ecosystems are still substantially harvested with nearly 1 billion people depending on reef ecosystems. However, evidence suggests that few of these fisheries are genuinely sustainable [REF]. The contributions ecosystems provide, whether material, cultural, or regulating, our most direct connection to the natural world. The Nature Futures Framework (NFF) invites us to reflect on our relationship with nature and the futures we wish to build. However, to chart sustainable pathways, it is crucial to understand the drivers of these contributions.

It is now clear that terrestrial communities are primarily impacted by habitat transformation and direct exploitation, while marine communities are predominantly affected by direct exploitation and climate change (Jaureguiberry, 2022). The contributions of communities are shaped by a complex interplay of driver: inherent factors, such as the latitudinal gradient of biodiversity; systemic changes, such as climate change; and more immediate and direct pressures, like resource exploitation and pollution. In this context, our exploitation and transformation relationship with nature stands out as the main lever we have for mitigating our ecological impacts in the short term and fostering more sustainable relationships with nature. Protected areas (PAs) have emerged as a key strategy for mitigating human impacts on ecosystems and safeguarding their contributions to nature and people [REF Diaz?]. At a time when many governments are committed to establishing protected areas (PAs) covering 30% of their lands and oceans by 2030, it is legitimate to ask ourselves what benefits PAs can deliver in terms of contributions to nature and people. But also to what extent could these benefits offset the ongoing ecological degradation driven by human activities?

To understand the negative or positive anthropogenic effects on community contributions, Species Distribution Models (SDMs) offer a powerful tool. Such models can predict contributions directly, bypassing the need to reconstruct entire species assemblages (Thuiller 2024, Deschamps 2023). A step further, joint SDMs (jSDMs), provide a better understanding of the synergies and trade-offs between multiple contributions by incorporating covariations among response variables while accounting for socio-environmental contexts (Paggiato 2023). Although modeling the impacts of human activities and climate change on species distributions and biodiversity is common practice, predicting patterns of multiple contributions as a function of social-environmental factors while considering synergies and trade-offs remains largely unexplored. These tools make it possible to understand the effects of protection plans and human activities on contributions, taking into account the local context. In addition to inference modelling, counterfactual scenarios allow us to explore the impacts of PAs and anthropogenic pressures by simulating alternative pasts. These scenarios enable researchers to reconstruct what ecosystems might look like in the case where an alternative past took place. For instance, by comparing protected and unprotected ecosystems, we can estimate the contributions of a protected ecosystem in a counterfactual scenario where no conservation measures were implemented. The difference between current contributions and those in this hypothetical scenario quantifies the conservation legacy of PAs—the benefits derived from protection, i.e. the benefits derived from protection. Conversely, for unprotected ecosystems, we can simulate a counterfactual scenario in which they experienced minimal human pressure, akin to that observed in ‘pseudo-pristine’ ecosystems, which are remote and relatively undisturbed. The difference between these simulated contributions and their current state reveals the human footprint on these ecosystems. For any ecosystem altered by human activity, we can then ask: How significant is our ecological footprint, and what steps can we take to minimize it? Alternatively, how substantial is the conservation legacy of protected areas, and how can it be amplified? An underlying question therefore emerges: Is the conservation legacy within protected areas comparable in magnitude to the human footprint left on unprotected sites?

Tropical reefs underline the urgency and importance of these questions. Although they account for a small proportion of the world’s marine surface area, these ecosystems are vital to almost a billion people through their fishing, nutrient supply and cultural heritage. Hosting about a third of marine biodiversity, tropical reefs are among the last ecosystems to provide direct support to local populations.However, their future is uncertain, threatened by overexploitation, pollution, and the accelerating effects of climate change. Understanding the impacts of marine protected areas (MPAs) on reef contributions - and considering scenarios of reduced anthropogenic pressure - could provide crucial guidance for conservation efforts and help foster a more harmonious relationship between people and nature.Using the case of fish community contributions in tropical reefs, we wondered: (i) What social-environmental factors drive fish contributions? (ii) What are the benefits of MPAs in terms of contributions? (iii) Are the benefits of MPAs sufficient to counterbalance the anthropogenic impact on contributions? To answer these questions, we took advantage of fish abundance data collected through visual census under the Reef Life Survey protocol across 2,480 reefs in tropical and subtropical oceans. We estimated 22 forms of contribution from fish communities and analyzed their relationships with 40 socio-environmental variables using a Bayesian hierarchical model. This approach allowed us to quantify the effects of protection and human activity on fish community contributions.

3 Results

3.1 Method summary

  • RLS data -> assessment of potential community contributions in each reef (i.e. at the site level: a GPS point at a given date): 2483 RLS sampled sites in tropical regions (see Figure 5), 22 contributions to Nature and People. (Inference of missing species traits to take into account most of the RLS sites, cf Methods)

  • Socio-environnemental data: 39 covariates of environment, habitat, and anthropic activities. (Inference of the missing data in benthic compositions (~30%) by Missforest )

  • Each RLS site is categorized according to its level of protection. Out of the 2483 sites, we had 655 out of reserve (no conservation measures), 1297 restricted mpa (all conservation measures), 531 full MPAs (no take mpa with high to medium compliance), spread over 33 countries.

  • Use of a Bayesian hierarchical model (Hmsc) to estimate the expected community contributions under different human pressure conditions, accounting for other habitat and environmental variables: use of the 39 covariates as linear fixed effects, country and site_code levels are used as random factors as (1|site_code/country).

Note

HMSC (Hierarchical Modeling of Species Communities) is a jSDM that relies on a latent variable approach to estimate the residual covariance matrix (Tikhonov, Opedal et al., 2020).

“In a multivariate model it is possible to define a random effect at the sampling unit level, as in a multivariate case the random effect models not only variation within species, but also co-variation among species, i.e. species-to-species residual associations.”

3.2 Inference of contributions

Model performance: half of contributions can be inferred with r² over 0.74

  • Good convergence of the model with thin = 2000, samples = 100, chains = 2: effective sample size around Nsamples * Nchains (see Figure 11), and good chain convergence (see Figure 15).

  • Good explanatory power of the full model with mean_r² = 0.69 (from 0.24 for calcium to 1 for available biomass), and a satisfying predictive power in a 5-fold crossvalidation with mean_r² = 0.33 (from 0.08 for calcium to 0.9 for mean endemism) (see Figure 14).

  • The full model outperformed the null model (only with random levels) which has an average predictive power of r² = 0.24 (see Figure 25).

Partitioning of explained variance: strong influence of ecological histories

→ Observations:

  • High importance of random levels. In average, 68.4% of the explained variance, 48.3% of the initial variance (see Figure 1 and Figure 15 (a)).

    • the potentially different intercepts for each random factor provide good explanatory power, as the random factors behave like latent variables (reproducing missing variables), but we couldn’t find obvious spatial pattern in latent variables (see Figure 22).

    • then, local effects seem very important, with contributions depending strongly on local contingencies. Much is determined by location: either because of the importance of the reef’s ecological history (species biogeography, particular past geological or ecological events, a local fishing method, a specific tradition of local populations,…), or because of local interactions between contributions (co-occurrence or inherent trade-offs due to micro-habitats, the presence of a coral type, a keystone species, …). Mean_endemism, functional_distinctiveness, omega_3, and evolutionary_distinctiveness are examples of contributions where the country random levels explain a high proportion of the variance, because these contributions are caracterized by large geographical patterns due to geological history or latitudinal gradients. Conversely, the contributions relating to biomass show a strong effect of the random sampling_unit factor, as they probably depend more on local factors and are more likely to co-occur.

  • At the global scale, we explain 31.6% of the explained variance (i.e. 20.8% of the initial variance) with fixed effects.

    • in average: 11.5% explained by environment, 10.3% by human variables, and 9.8% by habitat (Figure 15 (a)), with median SST over 5 years, and the human gravity as the two most influential drivers of contributions. (Figure 15 (b)).

    • Among the environmental variables, SST, chlorophyll and salinity are the three most important because they deeply affect fish communities. Among the human variables, gravity, marine ecosystem dependency and hdi are the three most important variables, as they must correspond to the degree and type of anthropogenic pressure applied to the communities. Among the habitat variables, the depth and area of terrestrial reef flat within a 500m radius are the most important, as they must correspond to the amount of space available for the communities.

    • In average, the MPAs protection_status explains only 1% of the variance in contributions.

Effects of anthropic covariates on contributions

→ Observations:

  • Among human covariates, gravity is the most important variables (~2.5% of the explianed variance, Figure 15 (b)). The presence of large population close from the reef (i.e. high gravity) has a strong negative influence on piscivores and available biomass, trophic web robustness and IUCN species (Figure 2). On the contrary, gravity has a positive effect on biomass turnover, aesthetics and evolutionary distinctiveness, as some areas with large populations, such as the Caribbean, constitute areas with a particular evolutionary history, have a low biomass that is rapidly renewed due to human exploitation, and feature relatively attractive fish communities.

  • Although reef protection explains only 1% of the explained variance, we found a positive effect on community aesthetics and public attention, available, invertivores and herbivores biomass, as well as species richness and the presence of IUCN species (see Figure 16 to see the support level of each estimates). …

Mantel statistic r=0.64: the same patterns of covariations are preserved, i.e. we couldn’t explain all covariations by fixed and random effects => importance of ecological history of sites, or inherent co-occurrence and trade-offs.

3.3 Counterfactual scenarios

In the end, we have only a few levers available at political level to increase contributions. But here, our levers are unbiased and we can examine what we can expect in the various contexts we observe today -> counterfactual scenarios.

These aren’t really predictive scenarios, because we don’t know the “activation energy” required to move from one evolutive path to another: we have to take into account the inertia of ecosystems. Here, we’re measuring the “ecological deficit of protection”, i.e. what we’d have now if we’d protected all the sites (with the current level/efficiency of protection of our sites). -> Taking action now should take us in these directions, but we can’t say how long it will take/ how easy it will be to reach these states. This kind of modeling helps to show what average gain we can expect.

→ Observations:

  • Worldwide, a high level of protection in unprotected sites could bring an average increase of 3.9% in contributions, with significant variability between contributions (from -3.5% for the biomass turnover to +24.7% for the herbivore biomass) and very high variability between sites (Figure 4). Overall, fish biomass and species richness would benefit the most from such protection, with an average gain of 14% in available biomass and 8% in actinopterygii richness. We also note a very strong gain of 20% in the presence of IUCN species, but with considerable variability, suggesting a strong contextual effect. The same contextual variability is found for piscivorous fish.

→ Observations:

  • We observed that all countries have similar responses to anthropogenic activities, with tropical reef communities generally richer in species, biomass, aesthetics, etc. if they are placed in conditions of high protection and low anthropogenic pressure (Figure 4 (A)).

  • However, we can see that these changes in contributions are largely influenced by the presence of human populations, in relation to reef protection (Figure 4 (B) and (C)). We see that the total protection of unprotected sites would result in a 16% of available biomass, whereas the reduction of anthropic pressure would let a 42% increase (see Figure 23 for changes in percent).

5.1 Filtering RLS data

Extract fish abundance assessement from RLS protocol, filter misidentifications, duplicates, and observations without fish

  • select “method 1, block 1 and 2” in RLS metadata (fish assessment in 2 bands of 5mx50m)

  • Remove all observations with misidentifications: “spp.”, “sp.”, “(cf)”, “hybrid”, “Unidentified”, “?”, “!”, “/”, “New”, “green form”

  • Same information recorded twice, by two different divers (same survey ID, block, species, total, and size) -> keep only one observation for each.

  • remove line with total = 0

filter fishes difficult to spot consistently with UVC

-Remove Elasmobranchs from abundance data (different behaviours, shy or not, …)

-Remove anguiliforms: 16 families according to WORMS: c( “Anguillidae”, “Chlopsidae”, “Colocongridae”, “Congridae” , “Derichthyidae” , “Heterenchelyidae” , “Moringuidae” , “Muraenesocidae” , “Muraenidae” , “Myrocongridae” , “Nemichthyidae” , “Nettastomatidae” , “Ophichthidae” , “Protanguillidae” , “Serrivomeridae” , “Synaphobranchidae”)

-Remove cryptobenthic families: c(“Aploactinidae”, “Apogonidae”, “Blenniidae”, “Bythitidae”, “Callionymidae”, “Chaenopsidae”, “Creediidae”, “Dactyloscopidae”, “Gobiesocidae”, “Gobiidae”, “Grammatidae”, “Labrisomidae”, “Opistognathidae”, “Plesiopidae”, “Pseudochromidae”, “Syngnathidae”, “Tripterygiidae”)

Checking size

-Filling values in Maximum length data: missing values are completed by applying the mean of the genus or the family (if no species in the same genus)

-Remove juveniles: all fish of 2.5 cm, and fish of 5 cm if the maximum length of the species is > 25 cm

-Remove abnormal size estimation: fish with a max length < 3.75, individuals for which size_class > 2*(Maximum length) or (Maximum length+50) => we removed all the surveys with size_class errors.

Filter outliers surveys

To avoid over-estimating fish abundance due to temporary fish aggregations or large roaming schools, we excluded surveys with a total biomass over 500 kg/500m² or an abundance exceeding 10,000 individuals.

Elasmobranch data

All Elasmobranch occurences are kept for the surveys meeting all the above conditions (Elamsobranch abundance isn’t used)

Protection status data

We classified sites according to the protection status (No take, restricted measures, out of MPA), and the compliance of populations to the management plan. Data providers assigned each MPA site from a high to a low compliance category, based on their expert opinion surveying those sites. No take MPA, associated with a high or medium compliance were considered under full protection, no take MPA wsith low compliance and all other protection status were considered under restricted protection.

5.2 Species traits

Species list

We consider only surveys that experience a minimum monthly sea surface temperature (SST) above 17°C, over the 5 years before the sampling protocol (survey date). +We consider only surveys between -30° and 30° of latitude to fit with the habitat variables of the Allen Atlas. We extracted all species (‘rls_species_name’) found in the remaining surveys.

Overall, we consider the abundance data of 1655 actinopterygians and the occurence data of 60 elasmobranchs.

Traits and databases

We extracted species traits from fishbase and others sources, to assess the contributions of each individuas. See supplementary for the table of traits.

Merge traits

To avoid taxonomic mismatch, we associated the species name of all databases and RLS data, with the accepted scientific name of rfishbase and its SpecCode (unique ID). All data base have been then merged using this unique ID.

Traits inference

Assess the performance of missforest on prediction: On 100 iterations, we create a trait dataset with 20% of NAs randomly placed. We used MissForest package (ntree = 100, max_iter = 10) to infer the created missing values and evaluated the prediction performance. Categorical variables were evaluate with accuracy (proportion of good prediction) and numerical variables were evaluated with R-squared correlation, over the 100 iterations. To fill the gaps, we ran 100 missforest models on the dataset and check the consistency of all models. Categorical values were kept if more than 80% of models converged to the same result. The median of the 100 numerical values were kept if the coefficient of variation (sd/mean) < 0.2, i.e if 1-CV > 0.8.

Targeted families

Fish families were classified at the word scale as fished (targeted), not fished (untargerted), or fished if species can reach 20cm (targeted > 20cm). See Supplementary for the list of families and the classification.

5.3 Contributions assessement

Cf “Fish communities can simultaneously contribute to nature and people across the world’s tropical reefs”, Flandrin et al. 2024, One Earth.

We assessed the potential contribution of a surveys only if the species for which the needed traits are still unknown represent less than 20% of the abundance and 20% of biomass of the survey.

We removed carbonate estimates and recycling because there were too much unknown values. Elamsobranch richness has been replaced by IUCN species. IUCN assessement for actinopterygians was extracted from Loiseau et. al 2024. Species aesthetic values from Langlois et al. 2022 were completed with knew species in RLS database following the same framework. Same for public attention from Mouquet et al. 2024. All contributions were scaled, and surveys with NAs were removed. Site contributions were assessed as the mean of survey contributions of a given date.

5.5 Hmsc: Hierarchical Model of Species Communities (Bayesian model)

-> “HMSC quantifies the explained variation within the observed communities through included fixed covariates, as well as through random effects, which can account for e.g. patterns arising from spatiotemporal processes. However, HMSC may include random factors that can be linked to species-specific co-occurrence patterns within the unexplained (residual) variation. These random effects are shown through either positive or negative pair-wise species associations reflecting ecological processes that take place in addition to species’ responses to the environment (e.g. dispersal limitation, biotic interactions, and random level specific covariates not captured by the fixed effects).”

-> multivariate response: take into account the associations between contributions: Random level at the sampling unit

-> we set countries and sampling unit as a random effects to account for the spatial structure of the data and potential co-occurences of contributions (interactions).

-> We fitted a normal HMSC model for all contributions because most contributions show normal distributions (Figure 6) and this gave the best prediction levels.

-> All contributions and covariates were log-transformed when they had right-skewed contributions, ad all contributions and covariates have been scaled to a mean of 0 and unit variance over the columns

-> Give posterior distributions of estimates and the explained variance partitioning.

-> Check Convergence, model performance and sensibility analysis: see supplementary.
